Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (GLRI)


USGS Hydrologic Support to Prevent Interbasin Transfer of Asian Carp at an intermittent connection of the Wabash and Lake Erie basins at Eagle Marsh, Indiana

Background/Problem

Eagle Marsh has been classified as a high risk pathway for Asian Carp migration from the Wabash River to Lake Erie. Adult bighead carp have been confirmed in the Wabash River basin for at least 15 years. The Wabash River basin connects with the Maumee River basin (Lake Erie basin) through a former glacial channel at Eagle Marsh in northeast Indiana during floods that occur after heavy rains and snowmelt. The Indiana Department of Natural Resources, Little River Wetland Project and Federal partners built a temporary adult carp barrier fence across a part of Eagle Marsh in 2010 to prevent movement of adult Asian Carp into the St. Mary’s and Maumee Rivers from the Wabash River when Eagle Marsh floods.

Real-time alerts of water levels were needed to indicate flooding conditions so that fence inspections could take place, identifying the presence or absence of adult Asian Carp in Eagle Marsh. Real-time information about water levels, streamflow directions and water temperatures was also needed in waterways that connect Eagle Marsh with the Wabash and Maumee River basins. This information is essential to designing permanent Asian Carp barriers that preserve use of the marsh as habitat and local flood relief, but prevent the expansion of this invasive species.


Description of Work

The USGS measures water levels at the adult Asian Carp barrier fence in Eagle Marsh and reports those data hourly to a USGS website. The barrier fence is an 8 foot tall, nearly 1,200 foot chain link fence across a part of Eagle Marsh. When flooding raises water levels at the fence, alerts are sent to State and local biologists and resource staff who respond and inspect the fence line for adult Asian Carp and debris accumulation.

The USGS also measures streamflow and water temperature at two sites downstream from Eagle Marsh to evaluate how precipitation in area watersheds causes streamflow and water levels to increase and how temporary changes in flow directions downstream on Graham McCulloch Ditch and Junk Ditch affect flooding and development of intermittent flow connections at Eagle Marsh.


Relevance/Benefits

USGS water monitoring provides data to design and maintain a barrier between the Wabash River and Maumee River basins at Eagle Marsh to prevent Asian carp from moving through this water pathway into Lake Erie. Plans by Federal, State and local partners to raise and widen an existing berm on Graham-McCulloch Ditch across Eagle Marsh and prevent Carp migration during floods depend on an understanding of how much water flows through streams that leave the marsh.


Key Findings

Water-level, streamflow and water temperature measurements and in-person observations after storms in 2013 identified how the Wabash River basin intermittently connects with the Maumee Basin (Lake Erie) during flood events at and near Eagle Marsh such that adult Asian Carp, if present, could bypass the current berm structures.

During some high water events, common carp were identified in the marsh in pooled water and at the temporary barrier fence. No Asian carp were observed.

Flooding in Graham McCulloch Ditch and Junk Ditch during June 2013, after a local rainfall of about 4-5 inches rain/24 hours produced a brief, several day flow connection at Eagle Marsh during a period when water temperatures (≥17-25 °C) were potentially favorable to carp movement.

Measurements on Junk Ditch identified how flood-related flows in the St. Mary’s River, a Maumee River tributary, reversed into Junk Ditch and into Eagle Marsh.
