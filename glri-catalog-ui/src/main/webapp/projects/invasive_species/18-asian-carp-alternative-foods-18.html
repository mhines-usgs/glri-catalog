Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (GLRI)


Alternative Foods

Background/Problem

Bighead (Hypophthalmichthys nobilis) and silver carp (H. molitrix), together the bigheaded carps, are filter feeding planktivores that have become increasingly invasive the Mississippi river basin since their introduction from China in the 1970’s. Recently, there is growing concern that bigheaded carps will most likely enter into the Great Lakes and undesirably affect the ecosystem and its valuable fisheries. However, due to removal of plankton by already established invasive dreissenid mussels, there has been some speculation that planktonic food supplies in some of the lakes would not be adequate for bigheaded carp growth and survival. Among the other effects of dreissenid mussels are increases in the relative abundance of undesirable bluegreen algae, increases in the abundance of anchored algae such as Cladophora, and very large concentrations of dreissenid mussel pseudofeces (undigested food particles) covering the substrate in some areas. In addition, larval dreissenid mussels (veligers) are locally extremely abundant in the plankton. In this study, we assessed the viability of pseudofeces, Cladophora, and veligers as alternative foods for bigheaded carps during periods of low plankton availability.


Description of Work

The viability of dreissenid mussel pseudofeces and Cladophora as possible alternative foods was tested for sub-adult bigheaded carp by offering the foods to the fish and monitoring their behavior (if they fed on these foods) and weight gain or loss. The viability of dreissenid veligers as a possible alternative food for bigheaded carps was tested by assessing their density and food value, and using a bioenergetic model to determine if bigheaded carps could subsist on veligers by feeding close to dreissenid mussel beds, where veligers are most abundant. Grass carp were also tested in this study.


Relevance/Benefits

Understanding the alternative foods for bigheaded carps is vital if preferred food, like phytoplankton or zooplankton, is in low quantities.


Key Findings

  • Bigheaded carps fed strongly on offered pseudofeces.
  • Grass carp fed strongly on Cladophora as expected, but sub-adult bigheaded carps ate only a little Cladophora . Bigheaded carp sub-adults fed Cladophora did not gain substantial weight but were able to maintain their body weight during the course of the experiment, whereas unfed fish lost significant weight. We conclude that bigheaded carps could eat Cladophora and that doing so would assist in survival during low abundance of preferred foods.
  • Silver carp and bighead carp both fed strongly on pseudofeces when it was present in the water column. Silver carp also fed on pseudofeces directly off the substrate, and bighead carp fed on plumes of pseudofeces in the water column caused by feeding silver carp.
  • Pseudofeces were low in energetic content, and would be a poor, although highly abundant, food for bigheaded carps In the Great Lakes.
  • On average, carp fed pseudofeces lost weight (not as much as unfed fish), but some individuals of both species gained weight. We conclude that pseudofeces would be an acceptable alternative food for bigheaded carps during periods of low plankton abundance.
  • Bioenergetic models indicate that, in the absence of other food, bigheaded carps would need veliger concentrations much higher than those found broadly in the Great Lakes, but much lower than veliger concentrations found near mussel beds during periods when the mussels are releasing veligers. We conclude that if bigheaded carps were to feed near mussel beds, veligers would provide substantial food, and intermittently provide enough food for rapid growth.
