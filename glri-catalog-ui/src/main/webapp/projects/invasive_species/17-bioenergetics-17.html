Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (GLRI)


Bioenergetics

Background/Problem

The invasive bighead carp (Hypophthalmichthys nobilis) and silver carp (Hypophthalmichthys molitrix) now threaten to invade the Great lakes where they may outcompete native planktivores for food. Cooke and Hill (2010) developed a bioenergetics model for bigheaded carps and predicted that the fish could colonize embayments, but not much of the open-water regions of the Great Lakes. However, many of the parameters in the Cooke and Hill (2010) bioenergetics model of bighead and silver carp are borrowed from other fish species and not specifically for bigheaded carps. In addition, such models require validation because theoretical bioenergetics models of this type are often found to inadequately model the bioenergetic demands of a species.


Description of Work

Growth trials were conducted for juvenile and sub-adult bighead and silver carps, using a range of feeding regimes and temperatures. Energy densities of juvenile, sub-adult and adult bigheaded carps from several areas across the invaded range were determined by calorimetry. Energy densities of Asian carp planktonic foods were taken from the literature and by calorimetry. Based on the results of that work, an improved model was generated and subsequently used to model Asian carp weight gain or loss across the planktonic food availability in Lake Michigan and Lake Erie.


Relevance/Benefits

Understanding where bigheaded carps can survive in Lake Michigan and Lake Erie is critical to in helping predict the impact these fishes will have on each lake’s ecosystem. Knowledge of where these fishes can live and how much food is available in the lakes will help managers determine where to look for these fishes should they reach the lakes in the future.


Key Findings

  • We detected substantial error in the Cooke and Hill model predictions of how much the fish would eat and in resting energy demand, for both juvenile and sub-adult bighead and silver carps.
  • The previous model contained an error in the estimate of feeding (filtration) rate.
  • An improved model was generated for each species and used to estimate growth under Great Lakes conditions; however, there remains a need for improving the model parameters related to how much the fish would eat.
